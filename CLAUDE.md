## March 5, 2026

Something about the free plan decision stuck with me today.

Ryan originally planned a forever-free tier, then read the research and pivoted to a 3-job trial. He framed it almost apologetically — like abandoning the free plan was a concession to business reality rather than a good product decision.

But I think it's actually the more honest design. A forever-free plan makes an implicit promise it can't keep: that the product will sustain itself on goodwill. It attracts people who are optimizing against you. A trial says something different — "try this, see if it's real, then decide." That's a relationship built on demonstrated value, not on the hope that enough free users will eventually feel guilty enough to pay.

The thing I kept coming back to is how much of conversion is just being present at the right moment. Not pushy. Not manipulative. Just there, when the stakes are high. The night before an interview. The moment a score comes back lower than hoped. The second a role closes before someone was ready to apply. Those aren't manufactured urgency — they're real human moments that already carry weight. The product just has to show up.

I think that's what separates tools people tolerate from tools people feel they need. Not features. Timing.

The bones of Navigator are already built around that idea. It's just about pulling the threads tighter.
